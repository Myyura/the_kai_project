# 京都大学 情報学研究科 知能情報学専攻 2025年8月実施 情報学基礎 F1-2

## **Author**
祭音Myyura

## **Description**
In the questions below, $\mathbb{R}$ is the set of all real numbers and $e$ denotes Napier's constant (the base of the natural logarithm function).

### Q.1
Consider the real-valued function $f(x, y, z) = xyz$ defined on $\mathbb{R}^3$. Derive the maximum and minimum values of $f(x, y, z)$ subject to the constraint that
$g(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - 12 = 0$. Derivations must be clearly shown.

### Q.2
Answer the following questions about the real-valued functions $f(x) = x \sin(ax)$ and $g(x) = \sin(ax)$. Let $a$ be a non-zero real number and $n$ be an integer greater than $0$. Here, $f^{(n)}(x)$ and $g^{(n)}(x)$ denote the $n$-th derivatives of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$, respectively.

(1) Derive $f^{(1)}(x)$ and $f^{(2)}(x)$.

(2) Derive $g^{(n)}(x)$.

(3) Derive $f^{(n)}(x)$.

### Q.3
Answer the following questions. Derivations must be clearly shown.

(1) Find the following limit, if it exists. If the limit does not exist, prove it.

$$
\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{x + y}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}} 
$$

(2) Determine the set of $x \in \mathbb{R}$ for which the following series of functions converges.

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{e^{nx}}{\sqrt{2n^4 + 4} - \sqrt{n^4 + 1}} 
$$

## **Kai**
### Q.1
Let

$$
f(x,y,z)=xyz,
\qquad
g(x,y,z)=x^2+y^2+z^2-12.
$$

Since the constraint set is compact, $f$ attains both a maximum and a minimum.

Using Lagrange multipliers,

$$
\nabla f=\lambda \nabla g,
$$

so

$$
yz=2\lambda x,\qquad
xz=2\lambda y,\qquad
xy=2\lambda z.
$$

If $xyz\neq 0$, multiplying these equations by $x,y,z$, respectively, gives

$$
x^2=y^2=z^2.
$$

Together with

$$
x^2+y^2+z^2=12,
$$

we obtain

$$
x^2=y^2=z^2=4.
$$

Thus $x,y,z\in\{\pm2\}$, and hence

$$
xyz=\pm8.
$$

Points with one or more zero coordinates give $f=0$, so they are not global extrema.

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{\max f=8}
$$

at

$$
(2,2,2),\quad
(2,-2,-2),\quad
(-2,2,-2),\quad
(-2,-2,2),
$$

and

$$
\boxed{\min f=-8}
$$

at the remaining four points with coordinates $\pm2$.

### Q.2
Let

$$
f(x)=x\sin(ax),
\qquad
g(x)=\sin(ax),
\qquad a\neq0.
$$

#### (1)
By direct differentiation,

$$
\boxed{
f'(x)=\sin(ax)+ax\cos(ax)
}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
f''(x)=2a\cos(ax)-a^2x\sin(ax)
}.
$$

#### (2)
The derivatives of $\sin(ax)$ repeat with period four. Hence

$$
\boxed{
g^{(n)}(x)
=
a^n\sin\left(ax+\frac{n\pi}{2}\right)
}.
$$

#### (3)
Using Leibniz's rule and the fact that derivatives of $x$ of order at least two vanish,

$$
f^{(n)}(x)
=
xg^{(n)}(x)+n g^{(n-1)}(x).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{
f^{(n)}(x)
=
a^n x\sin\left(ax+\frac{n\pi}{2}\right)
+
na^{n-1}\sin\left(ax+\frac{(n-1)\pi}{2}\right)
}.
$$

### Q.3
#### (1)
Consider the path $y=0$. Then

$$
\frac{x+y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}
=
\frac{x}{|x|}.
$$

As $x\to0^+$,

$$
\frac{x}{|x|}\to1,
$$

whereas as $x\to0^-$,

$$
\frac{x}{|x|}\to-1.
$$

Since the two values are different, the limit does not exist:

$$
\boxed{
\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}
\frac{x+y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}
\text{ does not exist}
}.
$$

#### (2)
Consider

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}
\frac{e^{nx}}
{\sqrt{2n^4+4}-\sqrt{n^4+1}}.
$$

Let

$$
d_n=\sqrt{2n^4+4}-\sqrt{n^4+1}.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{d_n}{n^2}
=
\sqrt{2+\frac{4}{n^4}}
-
\sqrt{1+\frac{1}{n^4}}
\longrightarrow
\sqrt2-1.
$$

Hence

$$
d_n\sim(\sqrt2-1)n^2.
$$

- If $x<0$, the root test gives

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}
\sqrt[n]{
\frac{e^{nx}}{d_n}
}
=e^x<1,
$$

so the series converges.

- If $x=0$, then

$$
\frac{1}{d_n}
\sim
\frac{\sqrt2+1}{n^2},
$$

so the series converges by comparison with $\sum 1/n^2$.

- If $x>0$, then

$$
\frac{e^{nx}}{d_n}
\sim
(\sqrt2+1)\frac{e^{nx}}{n^2},
$$

which does not tend to zero. Therefore, the series diverges.

Thus the set of convergence is

$$
\boxed{(-\infty,0]}.
$$
